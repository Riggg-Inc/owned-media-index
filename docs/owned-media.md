# Owned Media: Definition, Assets, and Channels

## What Owned Media Means at Riggg

Owned media is any content you create and any channel you control — where no third party can revoke your access, change the rules, or take your audience away.

The critical distinction is between the **asset** and the **distribution layer**:

- **The asset** (what you made) is always owned. A video file, a transcript, a clip, a written post — you created it, you own it, regardless of where it gets published.
- **The distribution layer** (where you put it) determines whether your *reach* is owned or rented. Your website and email list are owned reach. LinkedIn and YouTube are rented reach.

This means social content you write and design is owned media. The platform you post it to is not. You own what you make. You do not own the pipe.

---

## The Riggg Framework: 5Ps

Riggg organizes all owned media activity around five operational stages. The 5Ps describe **what you do**. The assets and channels below describe **what you make and where it lives**.

| Stage | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Produce** | Capture clean, flexible source media |
| **Package** | Turn source media into distributable assets |
| **Publish** | Get assets into the right channels |
| **Prove** | Measure whether the system worked |
| **Preserve** | Make the content library compound over time |

## Owned Assets by Stage

### Produce

Raw outputs from a recording session. Owned before any platform touches them.

- Video master files
- Audio master files
- Isolated tracks (per speaker)
- Raw footage and B-roll
- Session transcripts (raw)

Everything created to wrap and surface the source media. Fully owned content regardless of where it's distributed.

**Titles and descriptions**

- Episode titles

- Guest-share packages (clip + caption + instructions)

### Publish

Where the asset goes. This stage splits between **owned channels** (you control the infrastructure and the audience relationship) and **shared distribution** (you own the content, not the pipe).

#### Owned Channels

These are the only channels where losing the platform means losing nothing — because you own the relationship.

| Channel | What Lives Here |
|---|---|
| **Website** | Canonical episode pages, show page, blog, show notes, transcripts |
| **Email list / Newsletter** | Episode releases, audience nurture, evergreen sends |
| **RSS feed** | The authoritative feed origin you control (via Buzzsprout, Transistor, etc.) |
| **Podcast hosting account** | Feed management, analytics, distribution control |
| **Owned community** | Forum or community platform hosted on your own domain |

#### Shared Distribution

You own the content. You do not own the platform, the algorithm, or the audience relationship. If the platform disappears or changes its rules, the reach is gone.

| Platform | Content Type |
|---|---|
| **YouTube** | Full episodes, clips, shorts |
| **Spotify** | Audio and video podcast episodes |
| **Apple Podcasts** | Audio podcast episodes |
| **LinkedIn** | Posts, clips, carousels, newsletters |
| **Instagram** | Reels, clips, quote graphics, stories |
| **X / Twitter** | Posts, threads, clips |
| **Facebook** | Posts, clips, live simulcasts |

!!! note "A note on YouTube"
    YouTube is treated as shared distribution — not owned — for the same reason Spotify and Apple Podcasts are. Google controls the algorithm, monetization rules, and account access. A channel termination or policy change would eliminate the audience relationship entirely. The video files themselves remain owned assets.

### Prove

Data and reporting assets that live in your systems. Owned by definition.

- Session performance scorecards
- Per-episode metrics reports
- Benchmark comparisons
- Conversion event logs
- CRM attribution data
- Pipeline influence reports

---

### Preserve

The longest-lived owned assets. These compound over time and power AI search discoverability (AEO).

- Clean transcripts (indexed and searchable)
- Vector-indexed content library
- Structured show notes optimized for LLMs
- Schema markup / structured data
- Citation architecture
- Content theme and topic map
- Reuse and repurposing library

## How This Relates to PESO

The PESO Model (Dietrich, 2014) classifies media as Paid, Earned, Shared, and Owned. Riggg aligns with the spirit of that model but sharpens two points:

**1. Social content is owned; social platforms are shared.**
Gini Dietrich places social media in the Shared quadrant. At Riggg, we split the asset from the distribution layer. The post you write, the clip you cut, the graphic you design — those are owned media. The platform you post them to is the shared layer. What you create is always yours. Where it reaches is rented.

**2. The 5Ps replace the channel taxonomy as the operating model.**
PESO helps you categorize media types. The Riggg 5Ps describe how to run a media operation — Produce, Package, Publish, Prove, Preserve. Where PESO asks "what kind of media is this?", the OMI asks "what stage of the system are we in?"

---

## The Ownership Test

When evaluating whether something is owned media, apply two tests:

**Asset test:** Did you create it? → It's owned content.

**Channel test:** If the platform shut down tomorrow, would you lose the audience relationship? → If yes, it's shared distribution, not an owned channel.

Owned media is built on assets you create and channels where you hold the relationship. Everything else is rented reach — valuable, but not yours.
